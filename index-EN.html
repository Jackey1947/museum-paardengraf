In May 2010, the largest horse burial in Europe was discovered near Borgharen, north of Maastricht. Sixty-five warhorses, buried in a single long trench — witnesses to a 17th-century siege that had sunk deep into the ground.

The Discovery

A find no one expected

On a May day in 2010, archaeologists uncovered bone after bone during a soil survey by Consortium Grensmaas at the Pasestraat in Borgharen. Vertebrae, hooves, skulls — nearly all in one line. Each shovelful deepened the bewilderment.

Eventually the ground yielded 65 skeletons, arranged over a length of nearly 40 metres. The animals lay in unnatural positions with legs sharply pulled up or backs arched — due to rigor mortis, or the rough manner in which they were dragged into the trench.

"It was fantastic, unique. They were all jumbled together; the head of one between the legs of another. And every day more bones came out, it just wouldn't stop."

— Mat Delarue, amateur archaeologist

Open day at the excavation, summer 2010 — visitors and horses at the site

The horse burial caused a real frenzy in the summer of 2010. The open day was swamped with visitors. Even afterwards, curious people visited the site on their own — sometimes pulling at legs and making horseshoes disappear.

The find in numbers
  • 65 horse skeletons, stacked in one siege trench
  • The trench measures approximately 50 by 2 metres
  • Location: Pasestraat, Borgharen
  • Discovered during soil research for Consortium Grensmaas
  • Never before had such a large horse burial been found in Europe

The horses
  • Age: 4 to 10 years — young, fit cavalry horses
  • Slender and agile, not the heavy draft horses from the region
  • Isotope research proves: not from the Maastricht region
  • Fractures and traces of excessive violence — they came from a battlefield
  • An archaeozoological study concluded they were almost certainly warhorses

The central question

1632 or 1673?

Two sieges, one mass grave

Which siege caused the horses to perish? There are two competing theories, both supported by historical sources and archaeological finds.

1632
Frederick Henry
  • Siege against the Spanish
  • Maps show a cavalry redoubt at exactly this location
  • Sources report fierce cavalry battles near Borgharen
  • Ruled out after new C14 research
1673
Louis XIV
  • French circumvallation fits exactly at the site
  • Ten thousand cavalry horses at the siege
  • Most likely (Dijkman, 2023)

New C14 analyses of horse teeth restricted the lifespan to 1650–1800, eliminating 1632 as an option. Archaeologist Wim Dijkman then projected the circumvallation line of 1673 — the complete ring of earthworks and ditches with which the French isolated Maastricht — onto the site.

It matched almost exactly. The French buried their horses in the siege ditch they had already dug themselves.

"They only had to drag them in by their legs."

— Wim Dijkman, archaeologist
The research

The Mystery Solved

How Wim Dijkman cracked the puzzle

Wim Dijkman
Archaeologist · Maastricht

Former curator of Centre Céramique. He commissioned new C14 analyses at his own expense and published his findings at an exhibition about the French musketeers at Château de Vincennes — the Parisian castle where Louis XIV resided before Versailles.

The first official research pointed to 1794 as the most likely date — the year of the French Revolutionary siege. The crucial evidence: an 18th-century clay pipe bowl and a bronze buckle with horse harness, found at the bottom of the grave.

Dijkman found this evidence too thin. "I wanted to know for certain." New C14 techniques dated the horses between 1650 and 1800 — this ruled out 1632, but 1794 also didn't match the archaeological context.

Battle painter Joseph Parrocel depicts the siege of 1673, with Louis XIV on horseback and dead horses in the foreground

The breakthrough: using modern georeferencing techniques, Dijkman projected the circumvallation line of 1673 onto the current map. The circumvallation was a complete ring of earthworks and ditches with which the French completely isolated the city. The line coincided almost exactly with the location of the horse burial.

It is also known from the siege of 1673 that Louis XIV brought an enormous mounted force — an estimated ten thousand horses divided among hundreds of squadrons. Given those numbers, it is not surprising that "a number of them met their end here," according to Dijkman.

"Horses are treated with great respect in France. For the Sun King himself, these noble animals were moreover indispensable to his image as a great military commander."

— Wim Dijkman
Why is this burial so rare?
  • At other battlefield sites, horse bones were historically used as fertiliser
  • At the Battle of Waterloo (20,000 dead), not a single mass grave has been found
  • The remote location of Borgharen has left the burial undisturbed
  • Nowhere in Europe have so many warhorses been found together
